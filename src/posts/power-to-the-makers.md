# Makers and Merchants

Dear Designers,

You have a superpower and you don't even know it. 

----

The first website I ever built, I shared the link with a friend in Tokyo.

I was in New York. He was on the other side of the planet. And something I made — something that didn't exist that morning — was suddenly there, between us, visible to both of us at the same time.

It felt like turning a light on in a dark room.

Not metaphorically. Something shifted in how I saw the world. I started seeing everything around me — every sign, every object, every interface — as the result of someone's intentional decision. Someone made this. Which meant someone could make it differently. Which meant the world was not fixed. It was designed. And I could do that.

That feeling is the gift. And if you're a designer, you know exactly what I'm talking about.

---

You can make things that don't exist yet.

Not reports about things. Not decks about things. Not roadmaps or frameworks or alignment sessions about things. The thing itself. The artifact that someone picks up, uses, and either loves or doesn't. You can hold a future state in your mind and pull it into the present. You can give form to the invisible.

Most people cannot do this. Your CEO cannot do this. Your PM cannot do this. The investors whose opinions reshape your roadmap every quarter — they cannot do this.

This is not a small distinction. This is everything.

---

The people who can make things have always underestimated their power relative to the people who sell them.

This arrangement is as old as civilization. Artists and scientists produce. Merchants distribute, monetize, and eventually degrade. The merchant needs the producer to have anything to sell. The producer, somewhere along the way, forgot that.

Tech forgot about design. You can tell because the products show it. The apps are bloated. The onboarding flows are hostile. The interfaces bury what users need under what the business wants to measure.

But here's what that wreckage proves: design was never optional. When designers aren't in the room, someone else makes the design decisions. Engineers make them. Product managers make them. Merchants make them. The result isn't no design. It's design made by people who weren't trained for it, don't value it, and won't be accountable for it.

Bad products are not a designer failure. They are what happens when design is absent, ignored, or overruled.

Without you, they have nothing worth using. Without you, they have nothing to scale, nothing to monetize, nothing to eventually ruin. The leverage has always been yours. You just haven't been pricing it that way.

---

Designers get paid less than product managers. This needs to stop.

A PM decides what to make. That matters. But the decision is only worth anything if the thing you build is worth using. The PM's strategy lives or dies on the surface a designer creates. The artifact is where strategy becomes real — or doesn't.

Price accordingly. Demand parity. Not after you've proven yourself. As a starting position, because the work justifies it.

Demand conditions that produce good work. Not corporate lunches. A real creative environment. The space and flexibility to do the thing you were hired to do. A photographer doesn't shoot in a closet. A designer shouldn't create in a bullpen with Slack notifications and back-to-back standups.

And don't make things you wouldn't put your name on. This is not a moral position. It is a professional one.

---

Nobody is making you take on five projects when you can only do one well.

Say it plainly: "I can do one of these well or three of them badly. Which do you want?"

What happens next tells you everything. A company that pauses and makes a real choice understands what design requires. A company that hands you the three projects anyway has told you exactly what your role there is and always will be.

That's not a reason to feel bad. It's information. You can stay with open eyes or go with a clear conscience. Either way you're not surprised. Either way you made a professional choice instead of absorbing someone else's dysfunction and calling it your job.

I know this is easier to say than to do. People need jobs. Not everyone can withstand the threat that follows a boundary. If you can't right now, that's real and it's legitimate.

But if you can — if you have any margin at all to hold the line — you will come out the other side a stronger designer. You will have done work you're proud of. And you will have self-respect, even if the merchants never give you their respect.

That is not nothing. That is everything.

---

You can make the future.

Not abstractly. Literally. The interfaces people use to talk to each other, find each other, understand each other, build things together — those are designed. Someone decides what they look like, how they feel, whether they respect the person using them or exploit them.

That someone is you.

The world is full of products that are hostile, confusing, and unloved. That is a design problem. And it is solvable. You were trained to solve it. You have spent your career developing the judgment, taste, and craft to make things that work and that people love.

Remember the first time you made something and shared it with someone far away. Remember what that felt like.

That feeling was correct. It was telling you the truth about what you're capable of.

Get your swagger back. The world needs what you can make.
