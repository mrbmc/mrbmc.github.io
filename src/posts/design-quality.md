Last week, I had to downgrade a product design from tier one to tier two. Not because our design team lacked talent. The chipset couldn't handle the animations and resolution we wanted, and upgrading the hardware would price us out of our target market. This chipset limitation didn't kill the design—it forced us to decide which three interactions mattered most.

This illustrates a hard truth: market economics determine design quality more than designer skill.

Design quality is a feature you can offer customers. Some customers will pay for products made with more craft, durability, and care. Other customers won't make that investment and will buy products offering a lower standard of quality. Some people buy from Ikea while others buy from Room & Board. The quality you can achieve is almost always a product of who you're selling to and what you can charge. 

When you see a beautifully designed product, you're not necessarily looking at a more talented design team. You're looking at a company with more expensive customers. The most common limiting factor on design quality I've seen in my career isn't the talent of the design team or organizational processes. It's market dynamics. If you want to raise the quality of design in your company and product, you need to find a market of customers big enough to pay for it.

The project I'm working on provides a clear example of market effects. The product is similar to a smart thermostat. The market for thermostats can be divided into three tiers of design quality (quality as defined by the screen resolution, animations, gestures, shading, typography, build materials). Tier one includes Nest and Ecobee Premium. Tier two has Sensi, Honeywell T9, and Ecobee Lite. Tier three is the knockoffs, nonames, and nonsense. The product cost in each tier correlates with the design quality. Tier one costs three times more than tier three.

We initially designed for the first tier, but the chipset controlling this product can't render the animations, shading, or resolution we wanted. We could upgrade the hardware, but that pushes the price into a different customer segment. The barrier between tiers isn't design talent—it's what consumers will pay. The business levers of market size and unit costs control the design quality.

They're different businesses. My job as a designer is to solve the business problem within the constraints that exist. Designers who focus on raising quality without expanding the market often price themselves out of their current customer base. Some of the classic examples of this miss include General Magic, Juiceroo, Van Moof, and Google Glass. All of these companies aimed for impossibly high design standards without connecting those standards to paying customers.

The quality tradeoffs in SaaS and consumer apps seem more abstract than my thermostat example because indirect costs accumulate across more requirements—design systems, usability testing, brand alignment. But the underlying principle holds. Design costs that customers won't directly pay for will always take longer to achieve.

When you can't push design quality forward, examine the market first. Understand who you're selling to and what they'll pay before blaming people who "don't get design." The constraint usually isn't who understands design—it's who will pay for it.
