I learned to balance a broomstick on my chin and juggle when I was 14. The best thing about knowing how to juggle is embarrassing my kids in front of their friends.

The trick to balancing something is that you don't actually "balance" it in the sense of creating an equilibrium at rest. You continually sway the object back in a controlled manner, never letting it come to rest. You can correct balance more easily when you know which way the broomstick will lean next because you make it go that way. Be proactive, instead of reactive, to change.

Picture a Sealion balancing a ball on its nose. It's pushing the ball around rather than reacting to the ball tipping over.

<video autoplay muted loop src="/images/blog/sealion-balance-ball.mp4"></video>

Maintaining momentum also makes it physically easier to change direction than to overcome inertia. Baseball players take a few steps during each pitch so they can switch direction easily when the ball comes their way. Pro cyclists attack from behind to build momentum before the other rider can react.

---

Product Design has an analog to this in how often we change direction based on feedback. We change direction frequently, usually in response to gaining a deeper understanding of the problem and its constraints. We learn through feedback, research, prototyping, A/B testing, or presenting our thesis to temperamental stakeholders. Each new insight causes us to alter our trajectory. 

Think of feedback like a wave that has both frequency (how often you get new data) and amplitude (scope of the feedback). You should aim for a higher frequency of learning, even if the scope stays smaller, because you can react to it and control it more easily. Just like balancing a ball on your nose becomes easier when the wobble stays smaller.

![Continual Feedback](/images/blog/feedback-constant.png)

The advantage of putting things out there before they are "ready" is that the cost of change is *usually* much smaller. The effort of adjusting to change grows exponentially the longer you wait, or the bigger the study, test, or feedback cycle becomes.

![Continual Feedback](/images/blog/feedback-delayed.png)


People who've worked with me can attest to my bias for action. I champion sharing early and often, and I walk the walk. I share embarrassingly early drafts of my work with my peers, leaders, and team just to get ideas out of my head with real feedback and practice. **If you can't share something with a few people on your team, how can you share it with the whole world?**

None of this breaks new ground. Agile, Lean, Shape Up, and other methodologies all build on the same foundational principle: "take smaller, more stable steps." I just found that this metaphor of balance resonated with people when I shared it. It helped them zoom out their perspective when navigating ambiguity.

One common counterargument I hear: "You can't iterate from a match to a lightbulb. You need to take a big swing." I like to remind those people about the Apollo program, the iPhone camera system, and Alex Honnold's free solo of El Capitan. All of those successes came from iterative progress toward a goal. If you don't know what your goal is... well, go figure that out first.

Another caveat: if you collect low-quality feedback through sloppy methodology, ignore good feedback, or can't tell good feedback from bad, then you don't learn at all. That's a different kind of problem.

On your next project, try to collect a little bit of feedback a lot more often than you'd like, and let me know how it goes.
