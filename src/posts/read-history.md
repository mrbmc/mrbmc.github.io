Some years ago--never mind how long precisely-- I was having lunch with my team and the topic of autonomous driving came up. A pair of early-20s designers shared deep skepticism about the appeal of self-driving cars.

Designer A opined, “Yeah, maybe it’ll happen, but I love my car. I want to drive.”

Designer B added, “Yup. I do love driving.”

**Me, “I’m sure people said they love riding their horse.”**

The look of sheer terror on their faces was priceless. Not terror from the vision of the future, but terror from the confrontation of their self-image. These young designers considered themselves progressive modernists. Suddenly they found themselves defending horse-drawn buggies.

The best way to see the future is to study the past. Baseball scouts say that past performance is the best predictor of future performance. The best way to forecast which players will succeed in the future is historical analysis rather than contemporary comparisons. Performance at the most recent level is just one data point. Data in isolation means nothing. **A data point only has value in comparison to something else.** Comparing two points gives you a line of trajectory that points into the future. A player who was good in A ball, great in AA ball and Elite in AAA portends well for the future because they've demonstrated sustained growth. A player who is good in A ball, and good in AAA ball will be good.

Current events reported by modern media lack context and direction, and the meaning of what they report can be influenced by the incentive driving the reporter. A baseline understanding of history provides that context for understanding the impact of the present. The more history we study, the more data points we have to compare the present against. We don't fall for hype cycles when we have seen how previous events played out.

We understand that autonomous driving is inevitable because we've seen trends with mobility emerge over time. We have seen that improved capabilities coupled with lower maintenance always displaces incumbent technologies. Steam displaced horses, diesel displaced steam, electric displaced I.C.E., etc. It's a clear matter of when, not if. This trend of innovation displacing incumbents is a key thesis in "[Sapiens – A Brief History of Humankind](https://www.ynharari.com/book/sapiens-2/)" by Yuval Noah Harari.

There are still historical anomalies. The Renaissance emerged as a response to the Medieval period. This follows a historical pattern of cultural cycles. But coffee's role in that transformation would surprise most people. I was blown away reading "[The Devil's Cup: A History of the World According to Coffee](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/642646/the-devils-cup-a-history-of-the-world-according-to-coffee-by-stewart-lee-allen/)" by Stewart Lee Allen to learn just how much alcohol people consumed before the Ottoman's brought coffee to Europe thanks to the ottomans. People, including children, drank liters of beer and ale every day. The contrast of moving from a depressant (alcohol) to a stimulant (caffeine) had a profound effect on intellectual culture at the time. It was the trajectory of change.

Reading the headlines of our trade publications this month, you might think machine learning and AI can solve every business problem we throw at it. Every transformative technology creates winners and losers. The printing press democratized knowledge but destroyed scribal culture. Cars enabled mobility but gutted urban neighborhoods. AI will follow this pattern—some benefits, some costs, determined by how we deploy it. 

So if you want to see into the future, read a history book regularly instead of relying on contemporary self-help books. Here are four books that I've read and fully recommend:

[Worn – A People's History of Clothing](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/606856/worn-by-sofi-thanhauser/)  
By Sofi Thanhauser

[Sapiens – A Brief History of Humankind](https://www.ynharari.com/book/sapiens-2/)  
by Yuval Noah Harari

[The Devil's Cup: A History of the World According to Coffee](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/642646/the-devils-cup-a-history-of-the-world-according-to-coffee-by-stewart-lee-allen/)
