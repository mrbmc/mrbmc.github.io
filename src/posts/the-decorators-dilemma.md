# The Decorator's Dilemma

I had two app ideas I was genuinely excited about building. A virtual tour guide for bikepacking routes. A wildlife conservation survey disguised as a scuba diving logbook. Both stalled at the same line: building them meaningfully required AI-generated content derived from models trained on stolen data, and every vibe coding tool was ready to oblige. When I hit that ethical boundary, I stopped. Other people don't.

That moment is the whole essay in miniature.

---

Vibe coding is the promise that anyone can build anything. Describe what you want in plain language, and a model assembles it. No engineering degree required. No design experience necessary. Just describe, iterate, ship. The barrier to entry has never been lower.

That's true. What's also true is what's underneath it.

Most of the models enabling vibe coding were trained on content scraped without consent, attribution, or compensation. Writing, photography, illustration, code — collected at scale from creators who never agreed to contribute to a commercial product. The individual developer using these tools on a weekend isn't the villain. They can't see inside the supply chain. That invisibility is by design.

The artificially low cost of building is only possible because someone else is paying the real price. That someone is every creator whose work was taken without asking.

---

We've seen this before.

A $4 t-shirt is only possible because of a specific stack of policy decisions — agricultural subsidies, offshore labor, import tax structures — that hide the true cost of production. The shirt is cheap. The cost is real. It just gets paid by someone else, somewhere else, later. The result is an overwhelming explosion of consumption and waste. Fast fashion exists not because consumers are irresponsible but because producers made disposability artificially affordable.

Vibe coding is the same architecture. When the inputs are stolen, the product is cheap. When the product is cheap, it's disposable. We are at the beginning of an explosion of single-use apps — built fast, used briefly, discarded — made possible because the true cost of their creation was externalized onto creators who never saw a cent.

A single-use app is no better than a single-use plastic bottle. We just haven't named it yet.

---

This isn't an accident. It's a pattern.

In 1886, Santa Clara County v. Southern Pacific Railroad established the legal precedent that corporations hold the rights of persons. What it didn't establish was the moral accountability of persons. That gap — between legal personhood and moral conscience — is where externalized costs live.

In 1996, Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act gave platforms a second layer of insulation. Platforms aren't publishers, the law said. They're distributors. Not responsible for what users create, even when that content causes measurable harm. Social media companies scaled on the back of that provision, externalizing the cost of harmful content onto users, advertisers, and society.

AI training data is the third chapter of the same story. Corporations went looking for the loopholes and found them. The law didn't make this behavior acceptable. It just confirms that corporations will always seek and exploit the gap between what's legal and what's right.

---

So where is design?

Designers like to position themselves as ethical stewards of the human experience. We invoke Dieter Rams. We talk about designing for good. We write Medium posts about the responsibility of our craft. And then most of us ship whatever the PM asked for and wonder why business leaders don't take design seriously.

The uncomfortable truth is that designers don't lack a seat at the table because business leaders don't value design. We lack it because we've spent decades proving we won't say anything hard when it matters.

Vibe coding is a five-alarm fire in our professional house. The tools that are replacing us are built on the expropriation of the creative work we claim to champion. And our response, largely, has been to post aesthetic screenshots of things we built in an hour and marvel at the productivity gains.

That's not stewardship. That's decoration.

---

So what does real responsibility look like?

It starts with producers. AI companies and vibe coding platforms need to treat attribution as a first-class feature — not a legal footnote, not a terms of service clause, but a visible, auditable record of what went into the model and who created it. Extended producer responsibility is an established policy framework. We've applied it to packaging, to electronics, to pharmaceuticals. There's no principled reason it shouldn't apply to training data.

It continues with designers. Not posting about ethics. Asking hard questions in the room. Naming the cost out loud to business leaders even when it's uncomfortable. Refusing work that can't answer the question. The same way a packaging designer who never questioned single-use plastic was complicit, a product designer who never questions the supply chain of their tools is complicit.

The line between using a tool and endorsing its origins is thinner than most of us want to admit.

---

I stopped at the line. More of us should know it's there.

The single-use app boom is coming whether we name it or not. The question is whether designers show up as decorators or as the professional class that's actually willing to say something hard when it matters.

The tools are cheap. The cost is real. Someone is paying it.

It shouldn't keep being the people who made the thing in the first place.
